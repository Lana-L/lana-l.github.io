Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is a relatively recent term that explains that our brains are not static. Years ago, people used to think that a child is born smart or not so smart, and therefore their potential for success is fixed. Neuro research now shows that the more you use certain parts of the brain, the better connections in that part get, and therefore you get better at that thing. (Kinda like the rant I wrote in the EQ and IQ blog?)

Some way to grow these connections is to practise the tasks that you want to improve, such as puzzles, or painting, or talking, or writing. The best part of neuroplasticity is that we now know that recovery from neurological conditions is possible - for example, people re-learn to walk and talk after a stroke. The unfortunate truth is that those people who refuse to try may not recover as well as those who go as hard as they can. NZ rehab guidelines state that patients should undergo as much therapy as they are able to tolerate. If the person gives up early, the therapy may not be as effective. Rope Neuro seems like a good rehabber in Auckland.

Growth mindset is a term used to describe the idea that if people know the above about neuroplasticity, they are likely to keep going. For example, students who are told that their abilities are not set in stone are more like to try and get better results in their studies. My frustration with the term is that it's another buzz word that has been co-opted by "wellness gurus" - it's possible for a thing to exist and be factual without someone trying to give a motivational speech about it and charge you $600 a night for a retreat..
