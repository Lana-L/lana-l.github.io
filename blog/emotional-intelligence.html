EQ and IQ

IQ stands for Intelligence Quotient and for a long time it was thought to be an immutable measure of how smart someone is. Neckbeards to this day claim to be 160 IQ and part of Mensa. However, a lot of IQ 'beliefs' have been debunked.

All IQ tests are often a measure of how good a person is at taking tests, and so the score can be improved with practice. A lot of IQ tests also don't account for language, education, and environment, a lot of which can be taken for granted, and aging and just lack of use - for example doing something every day makes it easier, while not doing something for a while makes it very difficult. Then add to it age related issues like failing memory and dementia, or at the other end just not being taught the maths basics.

Research on neuroplasticity shows that IQ is not as set in stone as we used to believe. Also IQ measure is a deviation around the mean. So, definition IQ of 100 should be 'normal', but what happens when everyone becomes smarter? 100 becomes smarter now than it was 100 years ago. Raw scores on IQ tests for many populations have been rising at an average rate that scales to three IQ points per decade since the early 20th century (the Flynn effect).

All that aside, IQ is meant to measure how well someone is at solving problems, computing things, and using logic.

EQ is Emotional Quotient, a term created to echo IQ and to describe how well someone is at regulating their emotions and interacting with other people. It was a term coined in a pop sci book in the 60s and gained popularity in the 90s when the world was obsessed with self-help books.

Just like IQ, it's just a shortcut term to describe how well a person relates to others. And just like IQ tests can be practised for and improved, the concepts that make up EQ can also be practised and improved on, without falling into the chasm that is popsci and "personality tests" (also known as astrology for office workers).

Both speak to important points though - if we want to be better at solving problems, we need to learn how to solve those problems and practise solving them; if we want to get on with others and be likeable, we need to learn how to behave ourselves in public, and then practise interacting with other people.
