Problem Solving

While solving the challenges for the JS Kata, I got stuck at the very start. I just could not comprehend what was required of me. I tried googling and had a look in test file, and it just seemed all bizarre. I took a few steps back, went to make a cup of coffee, and suddenly it dawned on me that the first challenge was extremely basic - just declaring some variables. But that's how the TDD works - it sets out what is required and you just have to fill in the blanks to comply with those requirements. I felt frustrated that it was much simpler than it seemed, and it's just that I didn't understand the parameters of the challenge. But once I figured that out, it was easy sailing after that.

One of the problems in the kata was FlipFlop - a variation of ye olde FizzBuzz I assume. It seemed too simple, I am still puzzled why I passed all the tests on the first go. Some of the other puzzles were much harder - I especially struggled with working with objects inside of objects. I think the elegant solutions came easiest to me when I was working with arrays and strings - I just sort of get them somehow? Maybe because my previous role was in data analysis, which also involved a lot of working with strings. The particular elegance I enjoy is being able to daisy chain all the methods together, like .split().reverse.join() - very cool.

I feel pretty confident with my problem solving skills and using associated techniques. My initial approach is always to just try something and see what happens, then console.log it, then read error messages and google. Although not exactly talking to my rubber duck (which I do have on my desk), I do find that just typing out an issue as if I was about to send an email to someone does an equivalent job. Still, nothing beats talking to another person for real, especially those who have not been involved in the project - though they don't seem to share my enthusiasm for it. I'd probably be best pretending to talk to them in my head. Asking my peers or coaches for help would be on my list of last resort I think - I'd just be too embarrassed asking things that many people have asked before.

I am reluctant to ask for help when it's something I should be able to figure out myself. It just seems like wasting other people's time. In person it's much easier, but by the time you go through the effort of typing something up and posting it, you might come up with the answer yourself, or sometimes the other people answer immediately pointing out the error of your ways which is extremely demorilising. For example, I noticed an issue with a game addon and posted on their github, they instantly replied pointing to some obscure setting I didn't know about. I know the problem did not used to exist and I know it appeared after an update. But oh well, I guess this setting is a workaround, just not as good as it used to be. I wish I hadn't asked. So, yeah, I try not to do it too much - especially since everything on the internet is logged forever (just like this post is, sigh).
